=== book/chapters/01-moonshot/index.qmd ===
For roughly fifty years, processor generations improved through a familiar progression where device scaling regularly delivered faster, cheaper, and more energy-efficient transistors. Quantitative analysis gave architects a way to compare the resulting design choices. As scaling slowed, microarchitecture, parallelism, and eventually domain specialization provided the remaining gains [@HennessyPatterson2017QuantitativeApproach; @HennessyPatterson2019GoldenAge]. This shift is visible in fifty years of frontier microprocessor data. Clock frequency and typical power flattened in the mid-2000s, while transistor counts continued to rise and core counts turned upward (@fig-microprocessor-trends).

[CODE BLOCK OMITTED]


@fig-microprocessor-trends marks the end of a free lunch. The transistor count kept climbing, but once frequency and typical power flattened around 2005, the single-thread gains that had arrived automatically each generation stopped. The field recovered them first through parallelism and then through specialization, and each turn handed architects more of the machine to manage rather than inheriting it from the next process node. The figure's dotted rightmost era marks a prospective next turn, one where AI helps the architect co-design across those layers rather than tune each in isolation. Whether that help is real, and why the need for it is now pressing, is the subject of @sec-design-loop-no-longer-scales.

That shift multiplied the architectures, software interfaces, and physical constraints an architect must hold in view. New dataflows, memory systems, and programming interfaces appeared in quick succession [@HennessyPatterson2019GoldenAge], yet much of the coordination among workloads, compilers, simulators, RTL, and physical design still falls to architects.

Specialization also made architecture decisions more tightly coupled, where a new dataflow can simultaneously change memory traffic, compiler mapping, verification effort, and physical feasibility. As cross-layer dependencies multiply, architects must coordinate far more models, tools, and evidence to defend a choice. This pressure does not make AI necessary, but it explains why architects are now exploring AI assistance. The golden age established that specialization is now where the major gains live; Architecture 2.0 asks whether AI can help architects design those specialized systems under the resulting cross-layer constraints, shifting the unit of work from the finished artifact to the reviewable study that produced it.


AI is now being applied to the design process that produced those accelerators, including architectural design-space exploration and physical placement [@WangEtAl2026AgentDSE; @MirhoseiniEtAl2021GraphPlacement]. In this book, *AI* serves as an umbrella term spanning traditional machine learning for fast screening, smaller language models (SLMs) used for narrow, tactical tasks, and large language models (LLMs) used for broader reasoning, with substantial overlap in practice. An architecture project may combine several of these methods with simulators, compilers, EDA tools, and checkers to generate mechanisms, predict behavior, search design spaces, or choose experiments. The methods might also critique assumptions or repair artifacts. Not every project needs all these roles; one might require more promising alternatives, while another faces limits on simulator time, physical-design runs, or the effort required to interpret conflicting results. AI should improve the architecture work connecting a design prompt to a defensible decision rather than merely increasing the volume of output.

AI assistance is not free. Large language models add computational overhead, latency, and financial cost, so orchestrating an AI-assisted design loop requires weighing inference costs against the simulator time saved.

Consider an architect entering the following design brief into an AI system to test system intent rather than current model capabilities.

> **Lighthouse prompt.** Design a low-power, 64-bit RISC-V-based compute subsystem for an
> XRBench-class real-time mobile XR workload. Realize it as a vector-capable CPU, accelerator, or SoC block under a 3 W TDP target in a 3 nm-class LP mobile process, and return a design-space report with evidence and rejected alternatives.

We call this the *Lighthouse prompt*. No public result reviewed for this book establishes an end-to-end answer. Its scale exposes the architecture work that a smaller generation task would hide, providing a long-term target for judging partial progress.

This compact prompt is not yet enough to guide architecture work. The architect must still define the workload and baseline, choose meaningful alternatives, connect them to executable tools, and decide what evidence could support or overturn a recommendation. While AI may assist with each step, it can also produce candidate designs, scripts, and summaries far faster than reviewers can evaluate them.

The prompt does not imply a single model, invocation, or uninterrupted agent. A credible answer would combine multiple methods, representations, software components, tools, experiments, and reviews. The true object of study is the complete AI-assisted architecture process and the decision it supports, rather than the apparent fluency of any single generated response.

If a method proposes a thousand accelerators but architects cannot test why they differ, explain why most should be rejected, or state what would overturn the apparent winner, the extra proposals have not advanced the design. A fast analytical model could still help by screening out thermal-budget violations before they consume scarce simulator or physical-design time. The value lies in reducing expensive engineering work, not in the proposal count itself.

We use the same Lighthouse prompt throughout this material so that later chapters can change the method or the target stack layer without quietly shifting the workload, constraints, or standard of evidence.




::: {.callout-learning-objectives}

well-scoped
AI-assisted project. That means you can:

- **Context:** Distinguish established uses of machine learning in architecture from broader AI participation across architecture work.
- **Formulate:** Define the Lighthouse prompt in terms of workload, baseline, alternatives, constraints, and non-goals.
- **Explore and implement:** Identify meaningful alternatives and the executable artifacts and tool paths needed to compare them.
- **Evaluate and explain:** Specify experiments and observations that could support or overturn the result, then explain the architectural mechanisms involved.

=== book/chapters/02-design-loop-no-longer-scales/index.qmd ===
Architecture work is getting harder to finish, not because good ideas are scarcer but because each new design must hold more of the system at once. A modern project spans more design information, more software and physical constraints, more possible designs, and more expensive forms of evaluation than an earlier one, and it must keep all of that consistent long enough to defend a single decision. Much of the complexity an architect once inherited from the next process node now has to be built and managed by hand.

That growth is not a flat list of independent problems. The pressures build on one another as a system gains layers. Microarchitecture and multicore execution add policies and coherence, specialization tailors a block to a workload, composing specialized blocks into a system-on-chip or a set of chiplets adds integration boundaries, and the executable software contract adds compilers, runtimes, and tests that keep moving even after the hardware is fixed. Each layer carries the constraints of the one beneath it and adds its own, so a fair comparison must hold more of the system at once. This chapter works through those layers in order, then turns to a pressure of a different kind, the rising cost and irreversibility of the evidence needed to settle the resulting comparison.

As those layers accumulate, the difficulty is not simply that modern projects can generate more candidates.
Work becomes hard to complete when the project cannot keep its information
current, define useful and valid choices, afford decisive evaluation, review
the resulting evidence, or connect that evidence to a decision. These five failure modes, which the chapter returns to as a diagnostic once the layers are in view, require different remedies and can reinforce one another.

The Lighthouse prompt introduced in @sec-moonshot, a low-power 64-bit RISC-V mobile-XR compute-subsystem request that this chapter treats as the mobile XR subsystem example, makes that difficulty concrete by asking an architect to compare compute organizations for a real-time mobile XR workload within a 3\ W, 3\ nm-class mobile envelope. A useful answer must connect the workload and
software path to microarchitecture, memory movement, physical limits, and
verification. The short prompt therefore expands into many choices,
measurements, and reviews before it can support a decision.

AI can help with parts of that work. A system might collect design information,
propose alternatives, predict their cost, or organize tool results for review.
It can also move work rather than remove it. A system working from incomplete
context may strengthen the wrong comparison, while faster generation or
screening may send weak candidates into expensive evaluation and review.
The first task is to identify what is actually slowing the work. That tells the
architect where AI might help and what evidence would show that it did.

::: {.callout-learning-objectives}
After this chapter you can explain why familiar architecture work is harder to
complete and judge where AI may help. That means you can:

- **Explain** how specialization, software change, physical constraints, and verification make architecture work harder to coordinate;
- **Distinguish** missing information, unhelpful or invalid choices, scarce evaluation, overloaded review, and unclear decisions, then explain how these problems can compound;
- **Explain** why hardware proposals include an executable software path and why that path can change before and after release;
- **Identify** what engineering work a specific AI method could reduce;
- **Judge** whether AI reduced that work without weakening the evidence or shifting the burden elsewhere.


:::

=== book/chapters/03-architecture-20-framework/index.qmd ===
To address this challenge, we must formally define the iterative process where these decisions unfold.

> **Architecture design loop.** An architecture design loop is the repeated process of examining the current design, making an allowed change, running the relevant architectural tools, interpreting the result, and deciding whether to revise, reject, or continue. Each iteration retains enough information for another architect to see what led to the next step.

The design loop is the execution mechanism inside a bounded architecture study, which the next sections define as the unit of work it serves.

AI assistance becomes reviewable when the architecture question, executed
work, evidence, and next decision remain connected. The architect must bound
the task, state the claim under review, identify the legal design choices,
expose the relevant design state, name the architectural tools and checks, and assign
authority for the next action. Those elements keep the AI method, tool path,
reviewer, and decision owner focused on the same comparison. Assigning authority per
action keeps the loop agentic rather than autonomous, with an AI method holding
initiative to propose, execute, and reject candidates, while the rights to waive a check or commit resources stay with the named people who answer for the outcome.

No one would tape out a chip based on a single raw test score. Just as safety engineering uses standards like DO-178C [@RTCA2011DO178C] to build an explicit *Assurance Case* [@Kelly2004GSN], architecture requires a clear link between the goal, context, strategy, and evidence. Framing the architecture loop this way grounds the AI's role, establishing the architect as the certifying authority while treating the AI as a tool that generates evidence.

Give an AI system only a broad system goal and it may suggest plausible cache,
vector, or accelerator ideas. It cannot reliably know which comparison the
architect intends or which decision the results should support. If the
immediate task is to compare cache configurations, the system needs the
baseline, allowed cache choices, performance and power limits, simulator
settings, and evaluation budget. The architect must also state what would rule
out a candidate and who may act on the results. If any of this remains
unstated, the system can carry out a coherent comparison that is different from
the one the architect intended.

Architecture already manages complexity by making important relationships
inspectable. Architects use models, simulators, workload characterizations,
cost estimates, design rules, and reviews to reason about large design spaces
[@HennessyPatterson2017QuantitativeApproach]. The Architecture 2.0 framework organizes this familiar work into clear dependencies and revision paths instead of a rigid pipeline. An AI system cannot reliably
recover the assumptions connecting those artifacts from a prompt or scalar
score. Model choice therefore comes after the architecture task and comparison
are clear.

Each challenge points to information that architects must expose to an AI
method and preserve for review (@tbl-bounds-to-framework).

| **Architecture challenge** | **What must become explicit** |
| --- | --- |
| Missing or stale information | The study identifies the workload, design, tool, and version state available to AI methods and reviewers, along with the consequences a model is expected to estimate. |
| Invalid or inadequate choices | The legal design space and tool interfaces define which changes are valid and which tool paths can run them. |
| Expensive or inconclusive evaluation | Evaluation budgets, fidelity rules, and evidence records show what the available feedback can support. |
| More results than reviewers can settle | Rejection conditions and checks show when a candidate must stop, and the authority assignment names who decides. |
| No clear next decision | The recorded evidence limits the conclusion, and the named decision owner decides what action, if any, follows. |

: **From architecture challenges to required information.** Each row connects a
failure mode to information that architects must make explicit before an AI
system can help with the comparison.
{#tbl-bounds-to-framework tbl-colwidths="[38,62]"}




::: {.callout-learning-objectives}
After this chapter you can describe the structure of an AI-assisted architecture
study, explain what an AI method can and cannot do inside it, and determine what
must remain with an architect or decision owner. You can:

- **Bound:** State the study's workload, baseline, design space, constraints, non-goals, and intended decision.
- **Distinguish:** Separate the study, its artifacts, its architectural claim, and the design loop used when work iterates.
- **Connect:** Relate represented state and legal actions to tools, AI jobs, feedback, and evidence.

=== book/chapters/04-data-representations-world-models/index.qmd ===
@sec-architecture-20-ontology introduced the Architecture Design Loop. To systematically explore design spaces, an AI system requires a formal, explicit representation of architectural knowledge and project state. A representation is not just text or an embedding; it must capture structural constraints, Register Transfer Level (RTL) boundaries, configuration parameters, and fixed physical limits.

An AI system might speak fluently about computer architecture yet fail to act on a current design. Consider an AI system given our Lighthouse prompt to design a low-power, 64-bit RISC-V compute subsystem for an XR platform. The model may possess generalized knowledge of RISC-V instruction sets, branch prediction algorithms, and out-of-order execution pipelines. Without an explicit representation of the project state, however, it remains oblivious to the active constraints. It does not know that the architect recently updated the target eye-tracking workload trace, restricted the L2 cache capacity to meet the XR headset's thermal budget, or fixed the memory bandwidth to satisfy an area envelope. Demonstrating fluency in generating RISC-V concepts does not grant an AI the context to satisfy physical constraints.

General architectural knowledge is meaningless without the explicit bounds of the current design. To propose a viable change, an AI system must fuse its learned statistical priors with the concrete, versioned constraints of the specific project. The system must represent the design problem, express a legal architectural action, and predict its consequences. We must build verifiable frameworks where the representation tracks what the AI consumes and returns, so the AI accelerates the legwork while the architect commands the direction.




::: {.callout-learning-objectives}
After this chapter you can identify how architectural knowledge enters an AI system and how it combines with current project state to support a design change. Specifically, you can:

- **Explain** what counts as architectural knowledge, where it comes from, and how dataset structure determines what a model can learn;
- **Contrast** the mechanisms through which knowledge enters an AI system, from pretraining to explicit retrieval and update;
- **Distinguish** what is preserved by model parameters, retrieved context, explicit design state, execution histories, and learned cost models;
- **Represent** a bounded design problem, its mutable fields, fixed constraints, and the failed candidates that constrain search;
- **Predict** architectural consequences by mapping represented state and a proposed semantic action to a bounded prediction.
:::

=== book/chapters/05-architecture-environments-tool-interfaces/index.qmd ===
@sec-data-representations-world-models established how we must *represent* architectural state to an AI agent, ensuring our abstract models reflect actual hardware behavior. Once an agent proposes a design change (whether sizing a new cache hierarchy or adjusting a prefetcher) it must actually execute that intent to gather evidence.

In a traditional workflow, the architect manually builds the design, launches a simulator like gem5, a cycle-level architectural simulator, or Ramulator, a main memory simulator. The architect then parses the console output and debugs any segmentation faults or license errors. Scaling AI-driven design requires replacing human-in-the-loop debugging with direct, programmatic agent-tool interactions. However, giving an AI permission to execute a shell script is not an environment.

This chapter connects an AI's abstract intent to the stateful reality of computer architecture and Electronic Design Automation (EDA) tools. We will define how to structure the read, action, and return paths, handle asynchronous execution, distinguish between tool crashes and design failures, and distill massive simulation logs into dense semantic observations.

=== book/chapters/06-methods-generation-prediction-optimization/index.qmd ===
An accelerator study can stall for several reasons. The architect may lack viable array organizations, have too few simulator runs to evaluate proposed ones, or face a review backlog of inconsistent results. These conditions call for different assistance. A generator can propose legal array geometries, a predictor can estimate which geometry might reduce cycles or trigger an IR drop, an optimizer can choose the next configuration to evaluate, and a critic can find a missing comparison, after which a cycle-level simulator can test the selected configurations. The first method question is therefore not which model performs best overall, but rather what work prevents the bounded study from reaching its next defensible result. Current model rankings will age, while the distinction among missing alternatives, expensive evaluation, poor experiment selection, weak review, malformed artifacts, and disconnected tool work remains useful.

To make sense of these options, we must draw a strict line. **A role is the *job description*, and the method is the *tool* we hire for the job.** The same role can be filled by different methods depending on the hardware or software problem at hand. An AI role operates in a vacuum unless its output is tested against a physical simulator. We must replace generic terms like performance with specific physical constraints, such as routing congestion or thermal density, to measure the cost of being wrong.

Eight roles cover the work developed in this chapter. Generation proposes
candidates. Prediction estimates consequences. Optimization chooses what to
evaluate next. Critique identifies a weakness, repair proposes a bounded
revision, and verification checks a stated property. Explanation develops a
mechanism account, while coordination routes state and work. Combining roles
does not erase their different outputs or checks.

Capability and authority also remain separate. A successful tool call,
prediction, repair, or verification step demonstrates a capability within its
tested scope. It does not authorize a stronger architecture decision.

> **Method role.** A method role is the specific job a model, search procedure,
> tool, script, or person performs inside a design loop, together with the state
> it may read, the actions it may take, the feedback it receives, and the
> authority its output does not exceed.

> **Feedback budget.** A feedback budget records the amount, latency, cost, and
> fidelity of evaluation available across tool runs, measurements, failed
> attempts, and review.

An architect should choose a method according to the required job, available
feedback, and check that can challenge its output. A reviewer can then ask what
the method read or changed, what it returned, what resources it consumed, and
where the result went next.

::: {.callout-learning-objectives}
After this chapter you can choose an AI method for an architecture task and
state the limits of its role.
That means you can:

- **Match** generation, prediction, optimization, critique, repair, verification,
  explanation, or coordination to the architecture work that needs to be done;
- **Compare** roles by what they read or change, return, consume, and hand off;
- **Apply** hardware grounding and feedback budgets as tests on every role;
- **Compose** roles while preserving independent checks and the decisions that
  remain with the architect.
:::

=== book/chapters/07-feedback-verification-trust/index.qmd ===
This core dilemma highlights the difference between gathering data and validating an architectural decision, following a three-part flow. First, architecture transitions strictly require evidence, not merely feedback; a tool return must be typed and bounded to a specific claim. Second, the bottleneck in this process is proxy-mismatch and correlated errors, where fast surrogate metrics diverge from physical reality. Third, AI accelerates the generation of plausible but invalid proxies, demanding stricter independent oracles rather than trusting consensus among correlated models.

Consider a synthesis report indicating that an AI-proposed 3W RISC-V XR subsystem misses the declared clock target. The synthesis run returns feedback, while the versioned report recording negative slack under named constraints serves as a tool observation. This observation contradicts the bounded claim that the implementation meets the clock target under that flow and corner, yet it says nothing by itself about functional correctness, security, or every possible implementation. A reviewer records the timing claim as unsupported for the proposed RTL transition or unresolved pending a revised implementation, showing that a single report prompts different subsequent actions. From the review result, the team separately decides whether the failed candidate belongs in the project trajectory or a timing-surrogate calibration set, rather than automatically fine-tuning a model. Concurrently, the assigned architect decides whether to stop, revise the RISC-V XR subsystem, change the target, spend a more expensive check, or seek a bounded waiver.

::: {.callout-learning-objectives}
Readers of this chapter should be able to:

- **Distinguish** feedback, observation, evidence, review, knowledge update, and architecture decision;
- **Bound** evidence by claim, comparator, scope, validity region, provenance, and uncertainty;
- **Diagnose** proxy mismatch, confounding, nondeterminism, and correlated checks;
- **Judge** what protected evidence and public evidence can each establish;
- **Select** a decision-relevant next check under limited tool and reviewer capacity; and
- **Record** a dispute, bounded waiver, or reopening condition without converting a failed check into a pass.
:::

These objectives guide the architect in transitioning from merely running tools to formally structuring and defending the evidence those tools produce. They emphasize that while AI can rapidly generate candidates, the burden of verification remains on the human architect.

=== book/chapters/08-running-the-loop/index.qmd ===
This crux addresses baseline shopping, a practical trap in AI-assisted architecture. Generative AI accelerates the creation of architectural candidates, but because simulators are highly configurable and models are highly sensitive to phrasing, it is easy to subconsciously baseline shop by tweaking simulator parameters after the fact to validate a plausible AI candidate. It is equally easy to prompt shop by silently discarding dozens of failed prompts until one produces the desired architecture. In a real EDA environment, the penalty for guessing wrong is severe. Waiting eight hours for a physical routing failure consumes vastly more calendar time and limited FlexLM license checkouts than waiting ten milliseconds for a Python syntax error. This trial-and-error hides massive physical costs, and the design loop must be strictly bounded to prevent them.

Before any model or simulator output is known, the architect fixes the workload, legal array shapes, simulator configuration, comparison budget, rejection criteria, and decision owner. Fixing these choices prevents post-result goalpost moving and enforces discipline without claiming the choices were intrinsically optimal, serving as the baseline search strategy that an AI generative proposer must beat (@sec-methods-generation-prediction-optimization).

The experiment below is deliberately modest, yet it anchors back to our Lighthouse prompt from @sec-moonshot to design a 3\ W, 64-bit RISC-V-based compute subsystem for an XR platform. We specifically zoom in on the XR platform's spatial tracking (SLAM) subsystem, which relies heavily on dense matrix multiplication. One recorded model call proposes three legal systolic-array datapath shapes for a compact three-layer SLAM matrix-multiplication workload. A fixed aspect-ratio heuristic proposes three shapes under the same four-call simulator budget. SCALE-Sim 3.0.0 evaluates both arms under one frozen configuration [@RajEtAl2025ScaleSimV3], and a declared workload contrast then tests the model's mechanism prediction. While its scope excludes model benchmarking, full-chip XR evaluation, and tapeout, it illustrates how a bounded architectural question is rigorously executed. It evaluates whether a no-change architecture result, an equal-call tie, and an unsupported mechanism explanation can be reported cleanly without being recast as an AI success.

The experiment still reaches a reviewable result, although no nonbaseline shape clears the declared decision margin. The model and fixed heuristic arms tie, and the model's directional prediction fails its contrast. The recorded status-quo recommendation is to retain the 32 by 32 baseline for the five unique evaluated shapes and stop this bounded search. For context, an illustrative system might use specific square or rectangular datapath dimensions (e.g., 256 by 256) optimized for its target workloads; our 32 by 32 baseline similarly serves as a fixed reference point rather than a claim of global optimality among all fifteen legal shapes. Another reader can inspect the proposal and rerun the recorded simulator computation without mistaking that replay for independent validation of the workload, score, or simulator.

Most of this procedure is familiar architecture work where an architect defines the comparison, validates tool inputs, measures candidates, tests an explanation, and decides whether the evidence warrants a change. AI participation adds a proposal and explanation whose scope, output, budget, provenance, and authority must be carefully controlled. This integration neither changes what SCALE-Sim measured nor grants the model authority to decide what happens next.

::: {.callout-learning-objectives}
After this chapter you can run and review a bounded AI-assisted architecture
study. That means you can:

- **Separate** the architecture outcome, AI contribution, mechanism explanation,
  and stopping decision;
- **Lock** simulation parameters via scientific preregistration to prevent baseline shopping;
- **Validate** structured output, bound repair, and preserve rejected alternatives;
- **Challenge** a proposed mechanism with a declared distinguishing contrast; and
- **Distinguish** replay of the recorded computation from independent reproduction and validation.

=== book/chapters/10-loop-patterns-across-stack/index.qmd ===
@sec-running-the-loop provided a bounded control case. A 32-by-32 systolic array candidate was evaluated under deterministic, replayable SCALE-Sim measurements. The candidates were cheap to discard, and the architect stopped the study before implementation because the evidence supported retaining the baseline [@RajEtAl2025ScaleSimV3]. @sec-evaluating-agentic-architect then turned from the chip to the agent that drives such a search. This chapter widens the frame again, from one bounded study to the range of decisions an architect faces across the computing stack, where feedback latency, reversibility, and consequence vary far more than they did in that control case.

If that same proposed array geometry were instead used to alter an RTL interface, the evidence burden and decision rights would change entirely. The simulator returns quickly and candidates are reversible before implementation, but the next RTL transition sharply increases consequence and required check independence.

> **Operating condition.** An operating condition describes one property of the next decision, such as feedback latency, reversibility, interface span, consequence, or check independence. The architect uses the conditions that apply together to decide what an AI method may do, which evidence and checks are needed, how the AI's internal representation must be updated, and whether the result can advance. An operating condition is not a stack layer, a maturity score, or a property of an AI model.

Four recurring combinations of operating conditions organize the cases considered here:

- **Fast and reversible:** workload packets, compilers, kernels, runtimes, and other work with close tests and local rollback.
- **Model-mediated architecture:** exploration of accelerator, memory, interconnect, chiplet, and specialized designs using feedback mainly from proxies, simulators, or staged tools.
- **Cross-interface and deployment:** co-design, migration, and live-system work whose effects cross owners or whose telemetry is noisy and confounded.
- **High consequence:** RTL, physical design, verification, signoff, and other work where rollback is slow, expensive, or impossible after authorization.

These conditions overlap and accumulate. A kernel schedule may have fast feedback during tuning, cross an interface contract when deployed, and become difficult to reverse when hardware is frozen around it. A chiplet study can simultaneously be model-mediated, cross-interface, and high consequence. When several conditions apply, the stricter requirements of each accumulate.

::: {.callout-learning-objectives}
After this chapter you can classify the next decision before deciding what work to automate. That means you can:

- **Compare** what transfers from a bounded architecture study and what must be revalidated when the next decision changes;
- **Separate** design-time estimates, field observations, and improvements caused by continuing software enablement onto three distinct clocks;
- **Decide** whether delayed evidence qualifies for an update surface;
- **Revalidate** evidence, checks, permissions, and capacity limits when work crosses a new decision boundary.
:::

=== book/chapters/11-what-architect-owns/index.qmd ===
Models, tools, and specialists may perform bounded work, but Architecture 2.0 separates task execution from responsibility. An AI agent can synthesize a netlist in minutes, but a human architect signs the $10 million tapeout check. AI can absorb the execution burden while human architects own the final outcome (@fig-task-horizon). Named people or organizational roles remain answerable for defining the work, setting the evidence burden, authorizing exceptions, and deciding whether to advance.

[CODE BLOCK OMITTED]

The trend is why the boundary matters now rather than later. The length of task an agent can finish unaided has doubled roughly every six months, from seconds of work in 2019 to a full workday by 2026, steadily enlarging what can be handed off. What does not grow with it is answerability, which stays with the architect however much execution crosses the line.

This separation distinguishes delegated work from assigned responsibility. Work is delegated when another person, tool, or AI system is permitted to perform it. Responsibility is assigned when a named person or role must set the governing conditions, judge the evidence, authorize the action, and answer for what follows. The term *the architect* denotes an authorized architecture role rather than one heroic individual. Some rights may belong to a lead architect, an implementation owner, a safety or security reviewer, a product authority, or a review body. The key requirement is that those rights and interfaces remain explicit.

This chapter states that requirement as a current engineering rule. A validated check may acquire greater authority when its scope, failure behavior, audit path, and escalation boundary are known. The organization still owns the decision to grant that authority and the duty to revisit it.

::: {.callout-learning-objectives}
After this chapter you can:

- **Distinguish** operations that may be delegated to models, tools, or checks from the judgments that remain assigned to an authorized architecture role;
- **Determine** who selects the workload, objective, comparator, evidence burden, and stopping condition for a delegated study;
- **Specify** the shared infrastructure and team competence required to maintain the scope and lineage of an architecture decision;
- **Identify** the open technical and institutional problems that prevent the Lighthouse from becoming routine practice.
:::
