Published

July 14, 2026

“The purpose of computing is insight, not numbers.”

— Richard Hamming, Numerical Methods for Scientists and Engineers (1962)

Author’s Note: Richard Hamming, a pioneer in computer science, argued that computing should yield insight rather than raw data. The distinction matters here. AI-assisted architecture should help architects ask better questions, test alternatives, explain mechanisms, and defend decisions. Producing more candidates is useful only when it improves that work.

Guiding question
What capabilities must an AI-assisted architecture process combine to turn system intent into an efficient system design that an architect can defend with evidence?

For roughly fifty years, processor generations improved through a familiar progression. Device scaling regularly delivered faster, cheaper, and more energy-efficient transistors. Quantitative analysis gave architects a way to compare the resulting design choices. When scaling slowed, microarchitecture, parallelism, and eventually domain specialization provided more of the gains (Hennessy and Patterson 2017, 2019). The change is visible in fifty years of frontier microprocessor data. Clock frequency and typical power flatten in the mid-2000s, while transistor counts continue to rise and core counts turn upward (Figure fig-microprocessor-trends).

Specialization brought major advances, but it also multiplied the number of architectures, software interfaces, and physical constraints that designers must consider. The rise of machine learning accelerated this change. The authors of Project Brainwave described the resulting spread of GPUs, FPGAs, neural processors, and other specialized hardware as a “Cambrian explosion” (Chung et al. 2018). New dataflows, memory systems, and programming interfaces appeared in quick succession (Hennessy and Patterson 2019). Much of the coordination among workloads, compilers, simulators, RTL, and physical design still falls to architects.

Chung, Eric S., Jeremy Fowers, Kalin Ovtcharov, et al. 2018. “Serving DNNs in Real Time at Datacenter Scale with Project Brainwave.” IEEE Micro 38 (2): 8–20. https://doi.org/10.1109/MM.2018.022071131.

Specialization also made architecture decisions more tightly coupled. A new dataflow can change memory traffic, compiler mapping, verification effort, and physical feasibility at once. As the number of alternatives and cross-layer dependencies grows, architects must coordinate more models, tools, and evidence before they can defend a choice. While this historical expansion of cross-layer relationships introduces the pressure, sec-design-loop-no-longer-scales explains the mechanisms by which workload change, software dependence, evaluation cost, and coordination pressure make design work harder to complete. This pressure does not make AI necessary, but it explains why architects are exploring AI assistance.

Field note: Field Note: Multidisciplinary Design Optimization (MDO)
Observation: Hardware specialization tightly couples the stack. An AI cannot explore the design space effectively because optimizing one parameter (e.g., dataflow) cascades into breaking constraints across different, siloed simulation tools (e.g., thermal, synthesis, compilation).

Context: In Aerospace Engineering (e.g., NASA’s BLISS framework), designing an aircraft faces the exact same coupled constraint problem across Aerodynamics, Structures, and Acoustics. Instead of building a single “magic” monolith simulator, MDO orchestrates fast, machine-learning surrogate models for broad exploration, and reserves the expensive, siloed physics simulators only for final verification.

Lesson: This proves to architects that AI should act as the orchestrator of existing tools, not a replacement for them.

AI is now being applied to the design process that produced those accelerators, including architectural design-space exploration and physical placement (Wang et al. 2026; Mirhoseini et al. 2021). In this material, AI is an umbrella term rather than the name of one model family. An architecture project may combine several AI methods with simulators, compilers, EDA tools, and checking tools. Those methods may generate mechanisms or edit artifacts, predict behavior and risk, search design spaces, or choose experiments. They may also critique assumptions, repair invalid artifacts, or check results. Not every project needs all of these roles. One may need more promising alternatives, while another may be limited by simulator time, physical-design runs, or the effort required to understand conflicting results. AI should improve the architecture work between a design prompt and a defensible decision rather than merely increase the amount of output.

Imagine an architect entering the following design brief into a generative, tool-using AI system. The prompt tests system intent, not whether a current model can complete the design.

Lighthouse prompt. Design a low-power, 64-bit RISC-V-based compute subsystem for an XRBench-class real-time mobile XR workload. Realize it as a Tensor Core accelerator block under a 3 W TDP target in a 3 nm-class LP mobile process, and return a design-space report with evidence and rejected alternatives.

We call this the Lighthouse prompt. It is deliberately audacious. No public result reviewed for this book establishes an end-to-end answer. Its scale exposes the architecture work that a smaller generation task would hide and gives the material a long-term target for judging partial progress.

This compact prompt is not yet enough to guide architecture work. An architect must still define the workload and baseline, choose meaningful alternatives, connect them to executable tools, and decide what evidence could support or overturn a recommendation. AI may assist with each part. It may also produce candidate designs, scripts, and summaries faster than architects and reviewers can evaluate them or connect them to a decision.

The prompt does not imply one model, one invocation, or one uninterrupted agent. A credible answer would combine several methods, representations, software components, tools, experiments, and reviews. The object of study is therefore the complete AI-assisted architecture process and the decision it helps an architect make, not the apparent fluency of one generated response.

Suppose a method proposes a thousand accelerators. If architects cannot test why they differ, explain why most should be rejected, or state what would overturn the apparent winner, the extra proposals have not advanced the architecture decision. A fast analytical model could still help by screening out designs that exceed the thermal budget before they consume scarce simulator or physical-design time. The value comes from reducing the expensive engineering work, not from the proposal count itself.

We use the same Lighthouse prompt throughout this material. That lets later chapters change the method or the part of the stack under study without quietly changing the workload, constraints, or standard of evidence.

AI in computer architecture is not new. Researchers have used it for branch predictors, prefetchers, surrogate models, reinforcement-learning1 controllers, design-space exploration, and autotuning. Chen, Penney, and Jiménez survey this work component by component in AI for Computer Architecture: Principles, Practice, and Prospects (L. Chen et al. 2020). Predictive design-space exploration and tensor-program autotuning are two examples (Ipek et al. 2006; T. Chen et al. 2018). In most of this work, learning operates inside an artifact or performs a specific job within architecture work defined by people.

1 Reinforcement learning is a machine-learning method in which an agent learns to choose actions that maximize a cumulative reward signal from its environment, often used to navigate complex design spaces (Sutton and Barto 2018). In an AI-assisted architecture study, it is one way to search. Its proposals still require the same evidence and checks as those from any other method.

Recent generative and tool-using systems are being tested on bounded parts of that work, including proposing architecture configurations, invoking tools, and revising a search from returned results (Wang et al. 2026). These systems can also generate text and scripts, but those artifacts are only parts of the work that connects an architecture question to a decision. Our scope includes both learned components inside a design and AI systems used to conduct the work. In either case, the important question is how the method changes the evidence behind an architectural conclusion.

Architecture work has always moved through repeated analysis and revision. Architects frame a problem, choose abstractions, construct models or simulators, select workloads, explore alternatives, evaluate results, reject weak candidates, and revise assumptions. This work moves from intent and workload characterization to architecture decomposition, construction, evaluation, explanation, and decision. Consider an architect weighing a larger L2 cache for a stated workload mix. The architect might use gem5, a cycle-level architecture simulator, or FireSim, an FPGA-accelerated full-system RTL simulation platform, to sweep cache sizes and replacement policies across representative workloads (Binkert et al. 2011; Karandikar et al. 2018). They would compare miss behavior, execution time, and energy estimates, then explain whether locality, contention, or latency hiding caused the result. Simulators, compilers, profilers, synthesis tools, spreadsheets, dashboards, and design reviews all mediate the judgment about whether the evidence supports a cache change.

For generative and tool-using systems, it is clearer to describe each method by the architecture job it performs than by its model family. Figure fig-architecture-development-triad previews the three roles most often used to produce and select candidates. Generation proposes or edits architecture artifacts, prediction estimates behavior and risk, and optimization searches tradeoffs under constraints. Three supporting roles challenge and check that work. Critique exposes weak assumptions, repair corrects invalid artifacts, and verification tests whether a result passes the declared checks.

These jobs need not occur in a fixed order. Generation can expose a missing constraint. Prediction can show that the available evidence does not separate two candidates. Optimization can allocate scarce simulator or EDA runs, while critique and verification can disqualify the apparent winner. Repair changes the artifact and may invalidate earlier measurements.

To evaluate any of these contributions, an architect must be able to see what the system inspected and changed, how it obtained feedback, which observations counted as evidence, and which checks could reject its result. The responsible architect still sets the intent, chooses the abstraction and fidelity, interprets the architectural mechanisms, decides what evidence is adequate, and determines which decisions may be automated. The degree of automation can vary, but the study should state which decisions AI may make and which remain with people. Automating a step does not transfer responsibility for the architectural conclusion.

AI-assisted architecture extends a long history of design automation, but it begins from a broader statement of intent than earlier tools.

Figure fig-automation-ladder places this broader form of AI participation in the history of design automation. The dates are approximate. Each stage accepts a broader statement of intent and produces a larger artifact.

  2. 1990s–2000s (High-Level Synthesis): High-level synthesis generated RTL candidates from algorithmic descriptions and moved part of the tactical microarchitecture search into tools (Coussy and Morawiec 2008).
  3. 2000s–2010s (Domain-Specific Synthesis): Building on deductive program synthesis from the 1970s, domain-specific synthesis generated executable code from specifications constrained by domain-specific languages (Gulwani et al. 2017).
  4. 2010s–2020s (Agile Hardware Generation): Agile hardware generation used parameterized templates, such as Chisel and its FIRRTL intermediate representation, to create specialized SoC instances from reusable IP (Amid et al. 2020).
  5. Prospective direction (AI-Assisted Architecture): AI may help connect workload intent, software contracts, architectural alternatives, executable tools, experiments, and physical constraints inside a scoped study. The earlier stages remain essential. Logic synthesis, for example, became dependable because static timing analysis and equivalence checking could expose bad netlists. AI-assisted architecture draws on these established tools while connecting decisions made across stages. The evidence must identify which tool produced each result and which assumptions limit how that result can be used.

Reviewable evidence from iterative work should include the proposals considered, the results obtained, the candidates rejected or revised, and the decisions that followed. Different readers use this information for different reasons.

  • Researcher: Describe an AI-for-architecture paper by naming the architecture question, the role AI played, the artifact or finding produced, and the evidence and limits behind it.
  • Tool builder: Ask whether a harness keeps the inputs, settings, results, and failures that another method or engineer would need.
  • Author or reviewer: Ask what would reject the claim, why the measured mechanism supports it, and which decision it can justify.
Learning objectives

After this chapter, you can expand a compact architecture prompt into a well-scoped AI-assisted project. That means you can:

  • Context: Distinguish established uses of machine learning in architecture from broader AI participation across architecture work.
  • Formulate: Define the Lighthouse prompt in terms of workload, baseline, alternatives, constraints, and non-goals.
  • Explore and implement: Identify meaningful alternatives and the executable artifacts and tool paths needed to compare them.
  • Evaluate and explain: Specify experiments and observations that could support or overturn the result, then explain the architectural mechanisms involved.
  • Defend: State a recommendation, its evidence limits, and the conditions that would reverse it.
  • Compare efficiency: Treat efficiency as a multidimensional architectural claim rather than a single score.

1.1 Architecture 2.0 and Existing Practice

These review questions apply whether people orchestrate every step or AI participates in part of the work. Architecture 1.0 is shorthand for familiar, human-orchestrated design. The architect defines the problem, chooses models, uses tools, interprets feedback, and decides what to build. The Architecture 2.0 vision retains that practice while allowing AI to take part in more of the work (Janapa Reddi and Yazdanbakhsh 2025), as summarized in Table tbl-arch1-vs-arch2.

Janapa Reddi, Vijay, and Amir Yazdanbakhsh. 2025. “Architecture 2.0: Foundations of Artificial Intelligence Agents for Modern Computer System Design.” Computer 58 (2): 116–24. https://doi.org/10.1109/MC.2024.3521641.

AI may help interpret an initial request, characterize workload distributions, propose an architecture decomposition, construct artifacts, select and run evaluations, diagnose results, and prepare a recommendation. These are possible forms of assistance across several systems and methods, not a claim that one public system performs the complete workflow. This book organizes that participation into six activities: formulate, explore, implement, evaluate, explain, and defend computer architecture decisions. These are longitudinal learner capabilities, whereas intent, workload characterization, decomposition, construction, evaluation, explanation, and decision describe recognizable architecture work. They overlap, but they are not competing stage taxonomies. Producing a plausible artifact may help, but it does not complete that work.

As AI takes on more work, the study evidence must identify the inputs and artifact versions available to the system, the actions it may take, the feedback it received, and the candidates it rejected. The architect remains responsible for the intent, constraints, abstraction, evidence standard, interpretation, and final decision. This information lets another architect determine what happened and what the result can support.

In an AI-assisted cache study, a method might propose cache sizes and replacement policies, a surrogate2 could screen them, and a critic might flag invalid workload assumptions, but the architect still decides whether the evidence supports a cache change. The contribution might be a cache design, an architectural finding, a reusable method or dataset, or a decision supported by the available evidence.

2 A surrogate model is a cheap, data-driven stand-in for an expensive simulator. In a design project it lets a method screen many candidates quickly, but its score is only a proxy. A promising candidate still needs a higher-fidelity check before it can support a design decision.

Figure fig-design-loop-breaks shows the practical difference. Both sides iterate, and both depend on architectural judgment. The AI-assisted side makes the inputs, artifact versions, permitted actions, tool feedback, evidence, rejected candidates, and decision authority explicit enough for a system and its reviewers to use.

An AI system that calls a simulator, writes RTL, or summarizes papers still depends on sound systems engineering. Architects need to identify the likely bottleneck, account for memory movement and physical limits, compare tradeoffs numerically, and decide what evidence justifies the next step.

When AI participates, it should also present a concise rationale that connects its proposals, tool results, and rejected alternatives to its recommendation. That rationale is not evidence by itself. sec-feedback-verification-trust covers the criteria that make an observation relevant evidence for a bounded claim. A rationale gives the architect a structured account to inspect for missing assumptions, contradictions, or conclusions that the measurements do not support.

Safety engineering offers a useful precedent. Later decisions are checked against the assumptions, hazards, and evidence on which they depended (Leveson 2012). If a later workload changes the locality behavior, the architect can see why the earlier conclusion may no longer apply.

Leveson, Nancy G. 2012. Engineering a Safer World: Systems Thinking Applied to Safety. MIT Press. https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262016629/engineering-a-safer-world/.

These practices make an individual project easier to review. The larger question is whether they can support AI across a much greater share of architecture design.

1.2 The Lighthouse Expresses Intent, Not a Complete Specification

Engineers use moonshot for an ambitious goal that requires more than incremental improvement. X, the Moonshot Factory, describes it as the intersection of a large problem, a radical solution, and a technological breakthrough that makes progress plausible (X, The Moonshot Factory 2025). The full solution may remain distant, but the target should be specific enough to organize experiments and show which advances matter.

X, The Moonshot Factory. 2025. Our Blueprint for Moonshots. https://x.company/blog/posts/moonshot-blueprint/.

3 Early-1980s frontier microprocessors contained about \(10^4\) to \(10^5\) transistors. By 2021, frontier chips contained tens of billions (Rupp 2022). VLSI design was already outgrowing transistor-level practice. Mead and Conway made higher-level abstractions, design rules, and fabrication access part of a method that could be taught and shared.

Rupp, Karl. 2022. Microprocessor Trend Data. GitHub data repository. https://github.com/karlrupp/microprocessor-trend-data.

Patterson, David A., and David R. Ditzel. 1980. “The Case for the Reduced Instruction Set Computer.” ACM SIGARCH Computer Architecture News 8 (6): 25–33. https://doi.org/10.1145/641914.641917.

Computer architecture has comparable precedents. The Mead and Conway VLSI methodology3 joined abstractions and design rules to tools, fabrication access, and pedagogy (Mead and Conway 1980). The reduced-instruction-set program reshaped the hardware/software contract around quantitative evidence (Patterson and Ditzel 1980). Both efforts produced more than a single design. They changed the methods architects used and the artifacts they could share.

Architecture moonshot. An architecture moonshot is a difficult, long-term target that existing tools and design practice cannot yet address end to end. It seeks a major improvement in how architects use AI to formulate problems, explore and implement alternatives, evaluate and explain results, and defend decisions. Advances in AI, data, or tools must make at least part of the target practical to investigate without implying that the whole problem has been solved.

Figure fig-moonshot-venn separates the three conditions in the definition. The grand challenge is architecture work that current, ad hoc coordination cannot handle well. The radical target is a rigorous AI-assisted practice that changes how architects formulate, explore, implement, evaluate, explain, and defend designs. The enabling breakthrough is the combination of AI, data, and tools that makes part of that target practical to investigate now. None of the three is sufficient alone. Together, they identify an important architecture problem, a different way to approach it, and a practical place to begin testing that approach even though the complete goal remains out of reach.

Later chapters examine bounded parts of this target rather than claiming to deliver it whole.

The Lighthouse prompt, which asks for a low-power 64-bit RISC-V compute subsystem for mobile XR, fits all three conditions. Today, architects coordinate decisions across workloads, software, hardware, tools, and physical design. The long-term aim is for AI to contribute across that work while the architect remains responsible for the final claim. Advances in models, data, and tools make parts of the approach practical now.

To apply that ambition to the Lighthouse prompt, we first need to say what architecture and architect mean here. Architecture includes more than a microarchitecture, block diagram, or chip artifact.

Architecture. Architecture is the hardware-software contract and system organization that turn workload intent and technology constraints into a defensible system design. It includes instruction set architecture (ISA) and microarchitecture, memory and interconnect, accelerators and chiplets, compiler/runtime interfaces, physical-design constraints, verification, deployment, and the evidence used to justify choices.

Architect. The architect is responsible for the decision. An architect frames the problem, chooses the abstractions, sets the evidence standard, defines what can reject a candidate, and remains answerable for the committed design. One person may hold this role, or several people may divide the work. The division of work must still state who may make each decision and who gives final approval. Methods can contribute, but the architect remains responsible for combining the results into a recommendation and defending the architectural claim.

1.3 Architecture Questions in the Lighthouse prompt

Because the architect remains responsible for defending the final claim, they must first unpack the prompt into specific, verifiable architectural decisions. Figure fig-moonshot-prompt makes the scale of the Lighthouse prompt explicit. The prompt occupies only a few lines, but answering it requires choices about the workload, software contract, compute organization, memory, power, compiler and runtime, physical design, reliability, and verification. The bottom panel shows the work required to connect those choices to tools, evidence, and an architectural decision.

Start with the prompt itself. High-level intent first becomes questions and coupled design choices, not a generated chip artifact. The prompt asks the architect to compare several compute organizations for a real-time mobile extended-reality (XR) workload while respecting a 3 W thermal-design-power (TDP) budget and the limits of a low-power (LP) process. Its individual phrases bound different parts of the study. “64-bit RISC-V-based compute subsystem” establishes the ISA, ABI, and software contract. “XRBench-class real-time mobile XR workload” sets the workload, while “Tensor Core accelerator block” leaves several compute organizations open for comparison. The 3 W target and 3 nm-class LP process supply physical limits that can rule out a proposal.

The prompt asks for a design-space report, but the study may also produce software, models, RTL, layouts, prototypes, or a chip. How far implementation must go depends on the claim. The report connects those artifacts and measurements to the proposed design and explains why alternatives were rejected. The prompt deliberately leaves the accelerator microarchitecture, memory system, software integration, and evaluation tools open.

Progress toward the moonshot will require more than isolated demonstrations. SPEC-style benchmarks made performance claims more comparable by specifying workloads, run rules, and reporting standards. Shared simulators such as gem5 made architecture experiments easier to inspect across labs. FPGA-accelerated simulation platforms such as FireSim, community benchmark suites such as MLPerf, artifact-evaluation practices, and open tool flows have mattered because they let other researchers understand, rerun, challenge, and safely build on reported results (Standard Performance Evaluation Corporation 2017; Binkert et al. 2011; Karandikar et al. 2018; Mattson et al. 2020). The same principle applies here. Shared tasks, benchmarks, tool interfaces, evaluation methods, and evidence standards can make results useful beyond a single study.

The table is a first-pass checklist rather than the final review form. An answer is incomplete if it cannot identify its workload slice, software contract, physical assumptions, supporting observations, or decision owner. The rest of the book develops these requirements and turns them into a compact review form.

Each row points to engineering that the short prompt leaves unstated. To judge whether a candidate really fits the “3 W, 3 nm-class” constraint, architects need results from RTL, synthesis, floorplanning, timing, IR drop, leakage, thermal analysis, and signoff. Comparing the “RISC-V with vector or accelerator option” alternatives also means checking the compiler, runtime, libraries, generated code, and portability. The prompt is therefore a computer architecture problem rather than a prompt engineering exercise. A supported answer must connect decisions and evidence across the stack.

The workload and process phrases also need more detail than the compact prompt can provide. XRBench, a benchmark suite for mobile XR workloads, supplies a workload anchor rather than a vague application label (Kwon et al. 2023). End-to-end XR research shows how tightly the stages from perception to display are coupled. Mobile XR is therefore a system-level architecture target, not a single kernel to optimize (Huzaifa et al. 2021). AI may analyze dataset or trace distributions to find missing phases and coverage gaps, complementing profiling, trace analysis, and expert workload judgment. The architect remains responsible for deciding whether the selected benchmark and distribution represent the system claim. The process node is stated as a class rather than as a named foundry process design kit (PDK). Current mobile SoCs are publicly described in 3 nm-class technology, but the architect must still name the process, libraries, voltage assumptions, and signoff path (Apple 2024a, 2024b).

The architect might begin with an XRBench trace slice and software pipeline, propose vector-width, local-memory, or CPU/accelerator alternatives, and screen them with a cheap proxy. The architect can then reserve scarce compiler, simulator, RTL, or physical-design feedback for the candidates whose results could change the decision. That selection rule should be stated before the run, along with what the resulting evidence may support.

Within that workflow, generation might produce legal vector configurations, prediction might estimate their data-movement and power risks, and optimization might choose which configurations merit expensive evaluation. A proxy may rank the candidates one way, a simulator may overturn that ranking, and an EDA run may return timing paths, congestion, or rule violations rather than a single score. Search can also overfit4 a convenient proxy.

4 Overfitting, in machine learning, is fitting the noise and idiosyncrasies of a training sample so that performance fails to generalize to new data. An optimizer can overfit a proxy the same way, raising the proxy’s measured score by exploiting its quirks rather than improving the true objective. Architects should therefore test a promising candidate with stronger evidence before committing to it.

Those methods can narrow the work, but their output alone does not show whether a compiler assumption holds, whether the measured behavior follows from the proposed mechanism, or whether the remaining tradeoff justifies a commitment. The evidence should include the candidates, assumptions, measurements, and rejections that affect the claim. It does not need every internal model trace. The architect must resolve the remaining questions using the complete body of evidence.

1.4 Efficiency Claims Across the Design Loop

Even if an AI-assisted process can produce a candidate and report end to end, the efficiency claim still requires a scoped system comparison. The difference between a plausible candidate and a defensible design is especially clear in an efficiency claim. The Lighthouse subsystem cannot be judged from one score. Workload behavior, software support, data movement, physical limits, verification effort, and deployment conditions can all change which design is preferable. AI may help architects coordinate those choices, but the resulting claim still needs evidence for the stated workload and constraints.

Efficiency is a claim about how well a particular architecture performs useful work with limited resources. The claim must name the design, workload, and resource budget it covers. A faster design has not solved the problem if it exceeds the power budget, cannot be verified, or depends on fragile software assumptions.

Efficiency now spans more than performance and power. Classical computer architecture made performance quantitative while also treating cost and power as first-class constraints (Hennessy and Patterson 2017). Dennard-style scaling once made it easier to improve performance while keeping power density manageable (Dennard et al. 1974). When that scaling regime weakened, dark silicon (the share of a chip that must stay powered off to fit its thermal budget) and the limits of multicore scaling pushed the field toward specialization (Borkar and Chien 2011; Esmaeilzadeh et al. 2011; Hennessy and Patterson 2019). The energy cost of data movement made arithmetic alone an incomplete measure (Horowitz 2014). Warehouse-scale operation expanded the boundary to power delivery, utilization, networking, operations, and total cost of ownership (Barroso et al. 2019). Sustainability adds operational and embodied carbon to the comparison. These depend on energy use, hardware manufacturing and infrastructure, utilization, geography, and system lifetime (Gupta et al. 2021). An AI-assisted comparison must state the assumptions and evidence relevant to each efficiency dimension. The architect should also name the violations that would rule out a candidate.

A valid comparison must also account for failed and rejected alternatives, not only the winners. Keeping those results prevents later architects from reevaluating candidates that earlier evidence ruled out. It also shows which parts of the design space the claim actually covers.

Modern benchmark suites already reflect this broader view. MLPerf, a community effort to make machine-learning performance claims reproducible across systems, includes deployment scenario, latency, throughput, and accuracy in its comparisons (Mattson et al. 2020). Useful work must be evaluated against the resource that is scarce in the stated setting, whether that is latency, throughput, energy, power, area, cost, carbon, reliability, verification effort, engineering time, or risk.

The design, workload, scenario, and scarce resource together define what “efficient” means. For the Lighthouse system, a candidate that improves average throughput may still be a poor choice if it misses an XR frame deadline, exceeds the 3 W envelope under sustained load, or adds verification work that the project cannot absorb. Changing any of those conditions changes the claim and the evidence needed to support it.

Table tbl-efficiency-dimensions separates six dimensions that an architect may need to compare. The architect should select the dimensions that define the claim. Reading the rows together also shows where a gain may shift cost or risk elsewhere.

Two kinds of efficiency are present in this comparison. The proposed system must perform useful work within its resource limits. The study must also use simulator runs, physical-design experiments, verification effort, and expert review carefully enough to reach a reliable conclusion. Reducing the cost of the study is valuable only when the remaining observations can still distinguish the alternatives and expose the failures that matter to the decision.

These dimensions interact. In the mobile XR setting anchored by XRBench and ILLIXR, frame latency, energy, sustained power, and data movement are tightly coupled; a gain in one can easily violate a deadline or thermal limit in another. Better simulated performance can also be offset by added verification work. Lower energy may depend on fragile software assumptions, and a candidate that wins under a proxy may fail on a more faithful workload. The architect must explain which tradeoffs are acceptable for the stated workload and constraints. An efficiency claim must state its scope instead of relying on one attractive artifact or score.

These coupled requirements do not mean every architecture project needs AI. Traditional architecture methods remain effective when the workload, abstraction, feedback path, and proposed change are clear. Architects should first identify what familiar models, scripts, simulation, and expert review already handle well. They can then state what they expect AI to improve. The test is whether the assistance leads to a better system or a more reliable conclusion, not simply more artifacts.

The Lighthouse prompt makes this concrete. The requested subsystem must support a workload class, meet a power envelope, fit a technology assumption, interact with software, and produce evidence. The comparison must weigh energy, latency, memory traffic, programmability, verification, and deployment risk. A single scalar objective may help an automated search, but it cannot be the whole architectural judgment.

1.5 Existing Systems Cover Parts of the Design Loop

Current work has demonstrated useful parts of AI-assisted architecture under bounded conditions. Predictive design-space exploration uses simulated performance to train surrogate models that estimate behavior across a specified design space (Ipek et al. 2006). AutoTVM uses measured hardware execution to guide a statistical search for tensor-program schedules (T. Chen et al. 2018). In each case, the input, architecture job, feedback source, and bounded result remain clear. We use a cache subsystem throughout this text as a running example of how an architect might leverage these tools to rapidly evaluate varying replacement policies or memory hierarchies.

AlphaChip is a prominent physical-design example. Its inputs include a netlist and macro descriptions; its job is macro placement; its feedback comes from proxy rewards like wirelength and congestion. The original paper reported macro placements produced in under six hours, and later first-party reports said the method contributed to several TPU generations (Mirhoseini et al. 2021; Goldie et al. 2024; Google DeepMind 2024). sec-proxy-mismatch-metric-gaming-and-calibration examines the public disagreement about its comparisons and proxy evidence.

While these tools succeed at their individual tasks, they also expose the integration problem required to complete a whole architecture study. To move from isolated tool successes to an end-to-end design loop, the architecture community must learn from adjacent fields. The ML and science communities accelerated their progress by adopting shared baselines, standardized datasets, and rigorous artifact evaluation. For example, ImageNet drove the deep learning revolution by providing a common benchmark, while CASP (Critical Assessment of Structure Prediction) allowed AlphaFold’s capabilities to be systematically measured against objective, community-held standards. These happened in ML and structural biology, so we must adopt them in computer architecture. Shared tasks, public datasets, and open evaluation protocols will be essential to combine narrow tool use into a robust, reviewable AI-assisted architecture practice.

1.7 Open Research Questions

The Lighthouse prompt and the bounded systems discussed in this chapter leave five questions unresolved. Progress will require better representations, tool interfaces, methods, evidence, and decision practices.

  1. When does AI-assisted design work become an architecture contribution? An AI method may be novel without improving what architects can understand, build, compare, or decide. Another method may improve an architecture decision under hardware/software and physical constraints without introducing a novel AI technique. Reviewers must distinguish those cases. An architecture contribution can include improved formulation or decomposition, not only a candidate artifact. The work must advance an artifact, method, dataset, environment, finding, or decision that supports an architectural claim.

  2. How can we tell whether AI helped the architect understand the design? A candidate score does not show whether AI exposed a missing cross-layer dependency, changed the next experiment, connected measurements to mechanisms, or recognized why an apparent improvement will not transfer. The hard part is distinguishing an explanation that changes what the architect understands or does next from a fluent summary written after the result is known. Useful assistance must reveal a mechanism or support a decision rather than repeat an expected explanation. This is Hamming’s distinction between producing insight and producing more numbers.

  3. What evidence is sufficient for an AI-assisted claim when complete reproduction is impossible? Architecture results often depend on proprietary workloads, process design kits, training inputs, tool settings, or deployed systems that outside groups cannot inspect. Reviewers must decide which combination of public artifacts, matched baselines, sensitivity studies, independent checks, and limited-access review is enough to understand what AI contributed and how broadly the architectural claim applies. AlphaChip makes this problem concrete, but the same difficulty appears throughout industrial architecture research. Any answer must distinguish a credible deployment report, a reproducible public comparison, and a claim that remains unresolved.

  4. How can reviewers identify unsupported decisions in AI-assisted architecture work? Architecture reviewers already know how to inspect artifacts, baselines, and quantitative tradeoffs. AI participation adds questions about the inputs and artifact versions available to a method, the actions it was allowed to take, the alternatives it rejected, and the decisions reserved for people. Reviewers need enough of this information to reconstruct why a candidate advanced or failed and how the work reached a decision. They do not need every internal model trace, and proprietary tools may make full inspection impossible. But the inputs, actions, results, and human judgments that affected the decision must still be available for review.

  5. What should architects share so AI-assisted results can be compared and built on? If AI-assisted architecture remains a collection of isolated demonstrations like the bounded systems discussed earlier, architects will struggle to compare results. A common basis could include shared problem definitions and case studies, workload and tool environments, baselines and experiment protocols, and records of rejected alternatives. Independent groups need enough of this infrastructure to compare methods fairly, learn from negative results, and build on a finding after the original demonstration. It should support those uses without forcing every architecture problem into one fixed benchmark.

1.8 Conclusion

The Architecture 2.0 moonshot is not about replacing the architect; it is about elevating the architect’s capacity to navigate complex, multi-dimensional design spaces. The Lighthouse prompt illustrates that moving from system intent to a defensible system design requires far more than generating a plausible artifact. It demands a rigorous process that integrates performance, energy, reliability, and cost within strict physical and software constraints.

While current point solutions like surrogate models or AlphaChip demonstrate what is possible in narrow domains, the true challenge lies in end-to-end integration. To achieve this, the architecture community must embrace the rigorous evaluation standards and shared baselines that accelerated progress in the ML and scientific communities.

Ultimately, faster proposal generation only matters if we have the evidence and insight to distinguish good ideas from dangerous failures. The tools and models will inevitably evolve, but the core responsibility remains unchanged: the architect must frame the problem, demand robust evidence, and remain accountable for the final design. The following chapters will provide the foundational framework to make this AI-assisted workflow a reality.

As you move forward into the rest of the book, keep these core principles in mind. They form the basis for evaluating any new AI-assisted design technique.
